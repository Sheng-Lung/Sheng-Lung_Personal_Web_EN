Fourier Transform


  • Discrete and Short Time Fourier Transform, DFT & STFT

Discrete Fourier Transform（DFT） $$ X_k = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x_n \cdot e^{-i 2\pi k n / N} $$

Discrete Short-Time Fourier Transform（STFT） $$ X_{m,k} = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] \cdot w[n - m] \cdot e^{-i 2\pi k n / N} $$

The lowercase n denotes the index of a data point in the time domain when the time axis is displayed as the x-axis. Given that the total number of data points is N, n represents the position of each sampled point along the time axis, where n = 0, 1, 2, ..., N−1. In other words, n is the time-domain index.

The lowercase k denotes the index in the frequency domain when the frequency axis is displayed as the x-axis. It corresponds to the position of each frequency component, where k = 0, 1, 2, ..., N−1. In other words, k is the frequency-domain index.

X[k] represents the magnitude (or value) of the signal at the k-th frequency component after transforming the entire signal into the frequency domain. In other words, it is the value at the k-th tick mark on the x-axis. For example, when located at the 11th tick mark, k equals 11. The interval between each tick mark is given by: \( \dfrac{f_s}{N} \)

The actual frequency corresponding to the k-th frequency component is:

$$ f_k = k \cdot \frac{f_s}{N} $$

These frequencies start from 0 Hz and increase by \( \dfrac{f_s}{N} \) for each x-axis tick, up to the Nyquist frequency \( \dfrac{f_s}{2} \)

For example, suppose there are \( 500 \) data points and the sampling frequency \( f_s = 10\,\mathrm{Hz} \) . In the frequency domain, the frequency at the 11th x-axis tick （\( k = 11 \) ） is:

$$ f_k = 11 \times \frac{10}{500} = 0.22\,\mathrm{Hz} $$

  • Fourier Transform: Computes the inner product between the signal and sine waves of different frequencies from 0 to \( \dfrac{f_s}{2} \) , revealing the signal’s frequency components.

    Wavelet Transform


    • Wavelet Transform and Continuous Wavelet Transform, CWT

    The wavelet transform at a given scale 𝑎 and shift 𝑏 is computed as:
    $$ \mathrm{WT}_x(a, b) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] \cdot \psi_{a, b}^*[n] $$ $$ \psi_{a, b}[n] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{|a|}}\, \psi\left( \frac{n-b}{a} \right) $$ The result \( \mathrm{WT}_x(a, b)\) indicates how well the wavelet at a specific scale and position matches the signal. By varying 𝑎 and 𝑏, the wavelet transform provides a time–frequency representation of the signal’s energy distribution.

    The lowercase 𝑎 denotes the scale parameter in the wavelet transform. It determines the width of the wavelet: larger 𝑎 values correspond to wider (lower-frequency) wavelets, while smaller 𝑎 values correspond to narrower (higher-frequency) wavelets. In other words, 𝑎 controls the frequency resolution of the analysis.

    The lowercase 𝑏 denotes the shift parameter (or translation) in the wavelet transform. It determines the position of the wavelet along the time axis, allowing the wavelet to slide across the entire signal to analyze localized features. In other words, 𝑏 specifies the time location at which the signal is being analyzed.

    The symbol 𝜓 refers to the mother wavelet function. It serves as the prototype for generating all wavelet functions used in the analysis. By scaling (via 𝑎) and shifting (via 𝑏) the mother wavelet, a family of wavelets is created to match different patterns in the signal.

    The symbol 𝜓∗ denotes the complex conjugate of the mother wavelet. When the mother wavelet is a complex function, its complex conjugate 𝜓∗ is used in the computation to ensure correct analysis of both amplitude and phase information. If the mother wavelet is real, the conjugate has no effect and can be omitted.

    Here, \( \psi_{a, b}[n]\) represents the mother wavelet function that has been scaled by 𝑎 and shifted by 𝑏, and \( x[n]\) is the original signal.

    Wavelet Transform: Computes the inner product of the signal with a mother wavelet at different scale (𝑎) and shifts (𝑏) to reveal time–frequency energy distribution


    Continuous Wavelet Transform, CWT

    CWT means applying the wavelet transform to data, where the scale (𝑎) can vary continuously (e.g., 𝑎 = 1 , 1.1 , 1.2 , ...) and the shift (𝑏) can take small steps across the signal. This allows the original time–amplitude signal to be converted into a scalogram—a flexible time–frequency representation—showing energy at different scales.


    Adaptive Time–Frequency Resolution by Wavelet Transform:
    As scale ↓, frequency resolution ↓, time resolution ↑.
    As scale ↑, frequency resolution ↑, time resolution ↓.
